Are you sure you want optimality?

Why "optimal" solutions are more distractions than solutions.

For a first blog post, why not dive straight into controversy?

For somebody like me, who's expertise is predicated on the notion that I can take a real-world problem, model it in mathematical terms, and then return an optimal solution, optimality feels like an airtight case for employment if not an outright requirement. It sells well: "Give me your problem and I'll give you a solution that nobody can beat!" By definition, an optimal solution cannot be improved upon, and if you ask anybody who is in charge of running some kind of business, process, system, etc., each of them will want to convince you that they're operating "optimally." Maybe even more importantly, if that same professional has decided that for some reason their ward is not performing optimally, they probably won't tell their boss that they're seeking a consultant who promises suboptimal solutions.

There is a very easy technical case that can be made under special circumstances for suboptimal solutions: heuristic approaches for large, computationally complex problems (think 50 city travelling salesmen problems, or similarly difficult combinatorial problems) are widely accepted purely on the basis of how much time it can take to calculate an optimal solution.
